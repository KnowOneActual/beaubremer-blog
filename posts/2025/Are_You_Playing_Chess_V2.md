You know the moment. You are in a meeting or scrolling online, and someone says: "I am playing chess, not checkers."

It is meant to sound smart. But the phrase has two problems. It is a cliché. More importantly, it is based on a
misunderstanding of checkers.

#### The Game You're Insulting

Many people think chess is the only real strategy game. They dismiss checkers as a simple game for kids. However, this
view is incorrect. The rules of checkers are simple, but the depth is immense.

A key rule forces you to capture a piece if you can. This creates unique tactical choices. Skilled players use this to
set traps. They sacrifice pieces to force the opponent into a losing position.

Checkers is very complex. The game has about 500 billion billion possible board positions. That is 5 x 10^20. This
number is hard to imagine.

Computer scientists solved the game in 2007. The work required huge computing power. They proved that perfect play leads
to a draw. This shows how vast the game is.

#### A Master of a Complex World

This was the domain of legends like Dr. Marion Tinsley. He was a math professor and the best checkers player in history.

> Over a 45-year career, Tinsley lost a total of seven games and once defeated a top computer program. He was a quiet
> genius with strategic mastery. If anyone played a complex game, it was the man who achieved near-perfection in
> checkers.

#### The Irony of a Borrowed Boast

This highlights the irony of using the cliché.

- **They're using a hand-me-down line** to claim originality, often channeling Denzel Washington in _Training Day_.
- **They're trying to sound smart** by punching down on a game of incredible depth they clearly don't understand.

Real strategy does not need a catchphrase. It is quiet, deliberate, and proven by results. Smart people do not need to
tell you they are playing a different game. You can see it in their work.

Let your work do the talking. Play your own game, and leave the clichés to the parrots.
